Chapter 16 From Boom to Bust

Postwar Context (1918-1920)
  • Spanish Flu: Killed 675,000 Americans (22M worldwide); ~28% of U.S. infected; spread by wartime mobilization
  • Palmer Raids: A.G. Palmer arrested ~6,000 radical aliens after June 1919 bombings; discredited when May Day 1920 uprising never happened
  • Return to Normalcy: Harding's 1920 slogan — relief from flu, race riots, Red Scare, Wilsonian moralism; not nostalgia but a real promise of stability
  • John Maynard Keynes: British economist warned Versailles reparations were impossibly high and would produce a worse war; compared Wilson to a provincial minister overmatched by European counterparts
Harding Administration (1921-1923)
  • Andrew Mellon: Treasury Secretary; cut top rate 77%→24%, bottom 4%→0.5%; halved national debt $33B→$16B; argued lower rates paradoxically produced more revenue
  • Good appointments: William Howard Taft as Chief Justice; Herbert Hoover at Commerce; Mellon at Treasury
  • Teapot Dome: Interior Sec. Albert Fall secretly leased Teapot Dome (WY) and Elk Hills (CA) oil reserves for $400K bribe; first cabinet official imprisoned
  • Charles R. Forbes: Veterans Bureau head; massive theft and mismanagement of veteran services
  • Harry M. Daugherty: Attorney General; fraudulently released German assets seized during WWI
  • Harding died August 2, 1923 of a heart attack in San Francisco, before the full depth of the scandals emerged
Coolidge Administration (1923-1929)
  • Oath by kerosene lamp: Coolidge sworn in by his father (a justice of the peace) at 2:47 AM in Vermont farmhouse — no electricity or phone
  • 'The chief business of the American people is business' — Coolidge's governing philosophy
  • 1924 election: Beat independent Robert La Follette and Democrats too divided to even condemn the KKK
  • 1926 Philadelphia Speech: Defended the Declaration of Independence against Progressive revision — 'those who wish to proceed in that direction can not lay claim to progress'
  • Chose not to run in 1928 — left before the crash, preserving his reputation
1920s Economy & Culture
  • Coolidge Prosperity: 42% GNP growth; 4.7%/year avg; 26M cars by 1929; average income rose $6,460→$8,016; national debt halved
  • Henry Ford: Model T ($300); moving assembly line; 9,000 cars/day by 1925; paid workers double competitors; said 'history is bunk' yet built Greenfield Village
  • KDKA Pittsburgh (1920): First U.S. broadcast radio station; by 1929 60% of families owned radios
  • Lindbergh (1927): First solo transatlantic flight; NY→Paris; 3,600 mi; 33.5 hrs; Spirit of St. Louis; $25,000 prize; 200,000 in NYC to greet him
  • Gargoyle Journalism (Kenneth S. Lynn): Sensational mass-media depictions of the 1920s (e.g. Frederick Lewis Allen's 'Only Yesterday') based on upper-class urban experience, not the whole country
  • Pseudo-Event (Boorstin): Events manufactured by mass media; danger of the 1920s was celebrity meaning 'famous for being well known,' not for actual achievement
  • Booth Tarkington: Indiana novelist; Alice Adams (Pulitzer) depicted ordinary Midwesterners — the real 1920s, not Fitzgerald's glamour
Cultural Conflicts
  • Scopes Trial (1925): Dayton, TN; John Scopes tried for violating Butler Act (banned teaching evolution); Bryan vs. Darrow; Mencken covered it; fundamentalism suffered decisive PR defeat; complication: Scopes's textbook was openly racist and eugenicist
  • Prohibition (18th Amendment): Passed Dec 1917, in effect Jan 1919; a Progressive (not reactionary) measure; alcohol fell 50% but spawned Al Capone and organized crime; real damage: created laws millions refused to obey, eroding respect for law itself
  • Al Capone: Chicago crime boss; proof Prohibition gave organized crime a permanent foothold; in Detroit, illegal alcohol was the second-largest industry
  • 1921 and 1924 immigration quotas sharply restricted entry of Southern and Eastern Europeans
Hoover & the Crash (1928-1933)
  • Herbert Hoover: Iowa orphan → Stanford engineer → Belgium relief hero → Commerce Secretary; 'associationalism' (voluntary business-government cooperation); admired by Keynes; the Depression completely defeated him
  • Al Smith (1928): First Catholic major-party presidential nominee; lost to Hoover; signaled a new Democratic coalition forming
  • Stock Market Crash (1929): Black Thursday (Oct 24) + Black Tuesday (Oct 29); Dow 381→41 over three years; U.S. Steel $262→$22; the crash was a herald, not the principal cause, of the Depression

About the Declaration there is a finality that is exceedingly restful... If anyone wishes to deny their truth or their soundness, the only direction in which he can proceed historically is not forward, but backward toward the time when there was no equality, no rights of the individual, no rule of the people.

Calvin Coolidge · Philadelphia · July 1926

Chapter 17 The New Deal

Hoover's Depression Response (1930-1933)
  • Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930): Hoover called it 'obnoxious' but signed; raised duties on 1,000+ items; Europe retaliated; U.S. exports and imports cut in half
  • RFC (Reconstruction Finance Corp., 1932): Emergency loans to banks, railroads, and institutions; criticized for helping the wealthy while ignoring individual suffering; Will Rogers: 'money trickles up, not down'
  • Hoovervilles: Makeshift shanty towns of cardboard and plywood; named mockingly after Hoover; symbol of Depression's human cost
  • Bonus Army (Summer 1932): 43,000 marchers — 17,000 WWI veterans + families — demanded early bonus payment; camped at Anacostia Flats; Hoover sent in Gen. MacArthur
  • MacArthur disobeyed Hoover's orders and drove directly into the camp with tear gas and tanks; Sen. Hiram Johnson: 'one of the blackest pages in our history'
  • Hoover's 'red line': Refused to cross into direct federal-individual relief, believing it would destroy self-reliance and break the constitutional order
FDR & the First New Deal (1933)
  • FDR: Distant cousin of TR; educated Groton, Harvard, Columbia Law; paralyzed from waist down at 39 by Guillain-Barré; concealed from public; won 44/48 states in 1932
  • Oliver Wendell Holmes: 'A second-class intellect, but a first-class temperament' — FDR's unsinkable optimism was exactly what the Depression required
  • Brain Trust: Policy experts including Raymond Moley; conflicting ideas (antitrust vs. business-government cooperation vs. wealth redistribution)
  • Inaugural address (Mar 4, 1933): 'The only thing we have to fear is fear itself'; blamed 'money changers'; promised 'broad Executive power'
  • Fireside Chats: FDR's masterful use of radio to explain policies intimately to the American people
  • Bank Holiday: Declared on inauguration day; 4/5 of banks already closed; Banking Act of 1933 created FDIC (insured deposits to $5,000) and separated commercial from investment banking
Hundred Days Programs (Mar-Jun 1933)
  • NIRA/NRA (Gen. Hugh Johnson): Industries set codes for prices, wages, production; antitrust laws exempted; blue eagle emblem; captured by big manufacturers; struck down by Schechter (1935)
  • AAA: Paid farmers to reduce production to raise commodity prices; counterintuitive (destroyed food during a famine); large producers benefited; struck down 1936
  • CCC: 3 million young men in national parks and forests
  • CWA: 4 million workers building roads, bridges, schools
  • WPA (1935-1943): 8.5M+ employed; included artists and writers (Federal Theatre Project); unemployment still never dropped below 10%
  • TVA: Government electricity for impoverished Tennessee Valley
  • SEC: Regulated stock markets to prevent 1929-style speculation
  • FHA: Insured home loans, boosting construction and homeownership
Opposition Forces
  • American Liberty League (Right): Bipartisan opposition; led by both John W. Davis (1924 Dem nominee) AND Al Smith (1928 Dem nominee) — showing how far left FDR had moved
  • Huey Long (Left): Share Our Wealth Society; 7.5 million members; guaranteed income financed by taxing the rich; genuine threat to FDR; assassinated September 1935
  • Father Coughlin (Left): ~45 million radio listeners; promoted silver coinage; increasingly antisemitic
  • Dr. Townsend (Left): $200/month for all elderly (to be spent each month); inspired Social Security's old-age provisions
  • Milo Reno: Iowa populist; 'We were promised a New Deal, instead we have the same old stacked deck'
  • Schechter Poultry v. U.S. (1935): Supreme Court unanimously struck down the NIRA; opened the door to the Second New Deal
Second New Deal (1935)
  • Social Security Act: FDR's 'cornerstone'; retirement pensions funded by equal payroll taxes on worker AND employer (so recipients wouldn't feel like charity cases); plus unemployment insurance and aid for disabled/dependent children; first direct federal-individual financial relationship
  • Wagner Act (Robert Wagner, NY): Guaranteed right to organize and bargain collectively; forbade employer interference; created NLRB
  • Revenue Act of 1935 ('Wealth Tax'): Raised rates above $50,000; Hearst called it 'communism'; Chamber of Commerce withdrew FDR endorsement; raised little actual new revenue
  • 1936 election: FDR crushed Alf Landon 60.8-36.5%; won all but Maine and Vermont; African Americans shifted majority from Republican to Democrat for first time since Reconstruction
Second Term Decline (1937-1939)
  • Court-Packing (1937): FDR proposed adding justices; fiercely opposed by both parties; FDR backed down; two justices apparently shifted positions ('switch in time that saved nine') — upholding Wagner Act and Social Security
  • Roosevelt Recession (1937): Caused by FDR's budget cuts + $2B Social Security payroll tax drain + business fears; economy fell below early-1930s depths; gave ammunition to critics who said New Deal prolonged the Depression
  • Executive Reorganization Act (1937): Attempted to free executive from congressional oversight; defeated amid 'dictator' fears
  • 1938 Purge Primaries: FDR tried to oust conservative Democrats; failed completely; destroyed his aura of invincibility
  • 1938 elections: Democrats lost 6 Senate, 71 House seats, 12 governorships; conservative coalition (Republicans + conservative Democrats) controlled the House; Arthur Krock: 'The New Deal has been halted'
  • Conclusion: New Deal made permanent changes and restored national spirit, but unemployment never dropped below 10% — it would take war itself to end the Depression

The money was appropriated for the top in the hopes that it would trickle down to the needy. Mr. Hoover was an engineer. He knew that water trickles down. Put it uphill and let it go and it will reach the driest little spot. But he didn't know that money trickles up. Give it to the people at the bottom and the people at the top will have it before night, anyhow.

Will Rogers — on the RFC / trickle-down economics

Chapter 17 The New Deal

Key People — Chapter 17
Person
Franklin D. Roosevelt (FDR)
Distant cousin of TR. Born to wealth; educated Groton, Harvard, Columbia Law. Stricken at 39 by Guillain-Barre, permanently paralyzed below the waist. Won 1932 in a 44-state landslide. Campaign song: Happy Days Are Here Again.
Person
Raymond Moley
FDR's Brain Trust member. Said FDR never read serious books and had limited constitutional knowledge - compensated by extraordinary optimism. Later broke with Roosevelt over the Second New Deal.
Person
Hugh Johnson
NRA director. Blue eagle emblem: We Do Our Part. Named Time Man of the Year (1933). Asked Clarence Darrow to investigate the NRA; Darrow concluded it existed to help big business crush small competitors.
Person
Huey P. Long
Louisiana governor and senator. Share Our Wealth: guaranteed income financed by taxing the rich. 7.5 million-member Society. Genuine threat to FDR's 1936 coalition. Assassinated September 1935.
Person
Father Charles Coughlin
Detroit-area Catholic priest with about 45 million radio listeners. Promoted currency reform; denounced bankers in increasingly antisemitic tones. Initially FDR-adjacent, then fierce opponent.
Person
Dr. Francis E. Townsend
California doctor who lost savings in the crash. Proposed $200/month for all elderly (to be fully spent each month). His movement directly inspired Social Security's old-age provisions.
Person
Douglas MacArthur
Led troops against the Bonus Army. Disobeyed Hoover's direct orders by driving into the Anacostia Flats encampment with tear gas and tanks. Fatal image for Hoover.
Person
Robert Wagner
New York senator who sponsored the National Labor Relations Act (1935) - guaranteeing workers the right to organize and bargain collectively, creating the NLRB.
Person
Alf Landon
Republican governor of Kansas. Ran against FDR in 1936. Lost 60.8 to 36.5%, winning only Maine and Vermont.
Person
Milo Reno
Iowa agrarian populist. Supported FDR in 1932 but complained: We were promised a New Deal, instead we have the same old stacked deck. Declared opposition to FDR in 1936.
Person
William Randolph Hearst
Newspaper magnate who called the Revenue Act of 1935 communism. The Chamber of Commerce, which endorsed FDR in 1932, withdrew its endorsement in 1936.

Event

43,000 marchers - 17,000 WWI veterans plus families - demanded early bonus payment. When Congress refused, thousands camped at Anacostia Flats. Hoover sent MacArthur with military force. One of Hoover's greatest political disasters.

The only thing we have to fear is fear itself. Blamed bankers as money changers. Promised broad Executive power and war-like emergency measures. Followed by first fireside chat.

Event

Event

Sharp downturn from FDR's budget cuts, $2B Social Security payroll tax drain, and business fears of regulation. Dropped economy below early-1930s depths.
Event

Event
Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930)

Programs — Chapter 17
Program
RFC (Reconstruction Finance Corp.)
Hoover's 1932 emergency loan program for banks, railroads, insurance companies, building and loans, and farm-mortgage associations. Helped the wealthy while ignoring individual suffering. Will Rogers mocked it as trickle-down.
Program
Banking Act of 1933 / FDIC
Created the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (insured deposits to $5,000) to prevent bank runs. Separated commercial from investment banking. Preceded by bank holiday; followed by first fireside chat.
Program
CCC (Civilian Conservation Corps)
Employed about 3 million young unemployed men in national parks and forests. One of the most popular New Deal programs.
Program
WPA (Works Progress Administration)
1935-1943. Employed at least 8.5 million people building public works and supporting artists and writers. Unemployment still never dropped below 10%.
Program
NIRA / NRA
National Industrial Recovery Act created the National Recovery Administration. Industries set codes for prices, wages, production. Blue eagle: We Do Our Part. Captured by big manufacturers. Struck down by Schechter (1935).
Program
AAA (Agricultural Adjustment Admin.)
Paid farmers to reduce production to raise commodity prices. Counterintuitive during a famine. Large producers benefited over small ones. Struck down by Supreme Court in 1936.
Program
Social Security Act (1935)
FDR's cornerstone of the New Deal. Retirement pensions funded by equal payroll taxes on BOTH worker and employer. Plus unemployment insurance and aid for disabled and dependent children. First direct federal-individual financial relationship.
Program
Wagner Act (1935)
National Labor Relations Act (Sen. Robert Wagner, NY). Guaranteed workers the right to organize and bargain collectively. Forbade employer interference. Created the NLRB.
Program
Revenue Act of 1935 (Wealth Tax)
Raised income taxes above $50,000 plus corporate, estate, and gift taxes. Hearst called it communism. Chamber of Commerce withdrew FDR endorsement. Raised little new revenue.
Program
TVA (Tennessee Valley Authority)
Government-subsidized electricity for the impoverished Tennessee Valley. Created jobs and modernized the region.
Key Concepts — Chapter 17
Concept
Second New Deal / Second Hundred Days (1935)
1935 legislative flood after Schechter. More radical than First New Deal: Social Security, Wagner Act, Revenue Act. Driven by pressure from Long, Townsend, and Coughlin on FDR's left.
Concept
American Liberty League
Bipartisan opposition group resisting New Deal executive power expansion. Led by both John W. Davis (1924 Democratic nominee) AND Al Smith (1928 Democratic nominee).
Concept
Share Our Wealth Society
Huey Long's 7.5 million-member organization. Proposed guaranteed annual income financed by taxing the rich. Most serious left-wing challenge to FDR before Long's assassination.
Concept

Two Supreme Court justices apparently altered their positions after FDR's court-packing threat - upholding the Wagner Act and Social Security Act. FDR won the outcome but paid a severe reputational cost.
Concept
Conservative Coalition (1938)
Alliance of Republicans and conservative southern Democrats controlling the House after 1938. Blocked further New Deal legislation for decades.
Concept
Brain Trust
FDR's team of policy experts including Raymond Moley. Had conflicting ideas (antitrust vs. government-business cooperation vs. wealth redistribution).
Concept
Hoovervilles
Makeshift shanty towns of cardboard and plywood housing unemployed Americans. Named mockingly after Hoover. Symbol of the Depression's human cost.
Concept
Trickle-Down Economics (Will Rogers)
Rogers: money trickles up, not down - give it to the top and the people at the top will have it before night, anyhow. Enduring critique of supply-side economics.

Prompt 1
Compare and contrast Hoover’s and FDR’s responses to the Great Depression. What philosophical differences drove their policies, and why did FDR succeed politically where Hoover failed?
Show Key Points & Evidence
Hoover’s Approach
  • Associationalism: voluntary business-government cooperation — neither laissez-faire nor heavy regulation
  • RFC (1932): emergency loans to banks and railroads — helped institutions, not individuals; Will Rogers: “money trickles up, not down”
  • Hawley-Smoot Tariff: called it “obnoxious” but signed from party loyalty; Europe retaliated; exports and imports cut in half
  • The “red line”: refused direct federal relief to individuals — believed it would destroy self-reliance and break the constitutional order
  • Bonus Army disaster: MacArthur disobeyed orders, drove into Anacostia Flats with tear gas and tanks — fatal to Hoover’s image
FDR’s Approach
  • “First-class temperament”: Holmes’s assessment — FDR’s unsinkable optimism was exactly what the Depression required
  • Bank Holiday + Fireside Chats: restored confidence through direct, intimate radio communication
  • Hundred Days: FDIC, CCC, CWA, NIRA, AAA, TVA, SEC — unprecedented legislative pace with near-total Congressional cooperation
  • Crossed Hoover’s red line: Social Security created the first direct federal-individual financial relationship
  • Brain Trust: pragmatic, conflicting ideas — willingness to experiment rather than commit to one ideology
Key Contrast & Conclusion
  • Hoover’s principled refusal to provide direct relief vs. FDR’s pragmatic experimentation
  • Both failed to end the Depression — unemployment never dropped below 10%; only WWII did
  • FDR’s political success: 1936 landslide (60.8%); African Americans shifted to Democrats for first time since Reconstruction
  • Hoover was defeated by his own philosophy; FDR succeeded by abandoning ideological consistency
Prompt 2
How did opposition from the left — Long, Coughlin, and Townsend — push FDR toward the more radical Second New Deal? Cite specific programs and legislation.
Show Key Points & Evidence
The Left-Wing Challengers
  • Huey Long: Share Our Wealth Society — 7.5 million members; guaranteed income financed by taxing the rich; genuine threat to FDR’s 1936 coalition; assassinated September 1935
  • Father Coughlin: ~45 million radio listeners; promoted currency reform and denounced bankers; increasingly antisemitic; initially FDR-adjacent, then fierce opponent
  • Dr. Townsend: $200/month for all elderly, to be fully spent each month; his movement directly inspired Social Security’s old-age provisions
  • Milo Reno: “We were promised a New Deal, instead we have the same old stacked deck”
Schechter as Catalyst
  • Schechter Poultry v. U.S. (1935): Supreme Court unanimously struck down the NIRA — gave FDR the political opening for bolder legislation
  • First New Deal programs (NRA, AAA) were being captured by big business anyway — Darrow concluded the NRA helped big business crush small competitors
Second New Deal Legislation (1935)
  • Social Security Act: FDR’s “cornerstone” — retirement pensions, unemployment insurance, aid for disabled; responded directly to Townsend’s pressure
  • Wagner Act: guaranteed right to organize and bargain collectively; created NLRB — responded to labor unrest
  • Revenue Act (“Wealth Tax”): raised rates above $50K; Hearst called it “communism” — responded directly to Long’s redistribution demands
Conclusion
  • Second New Deal was more radical than First — driven by pressure from FDR’s left flank, not by FDR’s own instincts
  • 1936 landslide (all but ME and VT) validated the leftward shift
  • Long’s assassination removed the greatest threat, but his influence was already embedded in the legislation
Prompt 3
Explain how the cultural conflicts of the 1920s — the Scopes Trial, Prohibition, and immigration restriction — reflected deeper tensions between modernism and traditionalism in American life.
Show Key Points & Evidence
The Scopes Trial (1925)
  • John Scopes tried in Dayton, TN for violating the Butler Act (banned teaching evolution)
  • Bryan vs. Darrow: Mencken’s acid coverage gave fundamentalism a decisive PR defeat
  • Complication: Scopes’s textbook (A Civic Biology) was openly racist and eugenicist — the “science” side was not simply enlightened
  • Urban intellectuals (Mencken) vs. rural faith — but McClay shows neither side held the moral high ground entirely
Prohibition (18th Amendment)
  • A Progressive (not reactionary) measure — passed Dec 1917, in effect Jan 1919
  • Alcohol consumption did fall ~50%, but spawned organized crime: Al Capone in Chicago; in Detroit illegal alcohol was the second-largest industry
  • Real damage: created laws millions refused to obey, eroding respect for law itself
Immigration Restriction (1921, 1924)
  • Quotas sharply restricted Southern and Eastern Europeans
  • Reflected anxieties about national identity during rapid cultural change
The Deeper Tension
  • Gargoyle journalism (K.S. Lynn): sensational media portrayed the ’20s through an upper-class urban lens (F.L. Allen’s Only Yesterday)
  • Booth Tarkington: Indiana novelist (Alice Adams, Pulitzer) depicted ordinary Midwesterners — the real 1920s, not Fitzgerald’s glamour
  • Pseudo-events (Boorstin): mass media creating celebrity as “famous for being well known” — a fundamentally new cultural danger
  • All three conflicts pit a rapidly modernizing urban culture against traditional rural and religious America
Prompt 4
Assess the claim that the New Deal permanently transformed the relationship between the federal government and individual American citizens. What were the most significant changes, and what were their limits?
Show Key Points & Evidence
Before the New Deal
  • Hoover’s red line: refused direct federal-individual relief — believed it would destroy self-reliance and break the constitutional order
  • RFC lent to institutions (banks, railroads), never directly to people
  • No precedent for a direct federal financial relationship with ordinary citizens
Transformative Programs
  • Social Security Act (1935): first direct federal-individual financial relationship; payroll taxes on both worker and employer so recipients wouldn’t feel like charity cases
  • FDIC: government insuring individual bank deposits up to $5,000 — ended the psychology of bank runs
  • WPA/CCC: direct federal employment of millions (8.5M+ through WPA; 3M through CCC)
  • Wagner Act: federal government guaranteeing labor rights; created NLRB to enforce them
  • FHA: insured home loans, making homeownership accessible to millions
Limits of the Transformation
  • Unemployment never dropped below 10% — only WWII mobilization ended the Depression
  • Court-packing (1937): bipartisan opposition showed limits of executive power; FDR backed down
  • Roosevelt Recession (1937): budget cuts + SS payroll tax drain + business fears reversed recovery
  • Conservative Coalition (1938): Republicans + conservative southern Democrats halted further legislation; Krock: “The New Deal has been halted”
  • 1938 Purge Primaries: FDR tried to oust conservative Democrats; failed completely; destroyed his aura of invincibility
Conclusion
  • New Deal made permanent structural changes (Social Security, FDIC, NLRB still exist) and restored national spirit
  • But did not end the Depression or establish unlimited executive authority
  • Established the principle that the federal government has a direct responsibility to individual citizens — a line that, once crossed, was never retreated from
Prompt 5
How did Hoover’s philosophy of associationalism shape both his response to the Depression and its ultimate failure?
Show Key Points & Evidence
Hoover’s Background & Philosophy
  • Iowa orphan → Stanford engineer → Belgium relief hero → Commerce Secretary under Harding and Coolidge
  • Associationalism: voluntary cooperation between business and government — a middle path between laissez-faire and state control
  • Admired by Keynes; seen as the most capable man in government; not a do-nothing president
Associationalism in Action
  • RFC (1932): emergency loans to banks, railroads, institutions — voluntary-cooperation model applied to crisis
  • Urged businesses to maintain wages and production voluntarily
  • Hawley-Smoot: signed despite calling it “obnoxious” — party loyalty overrode his own judgment; Europe retaliated and trade collapsed
  • Expanded public works spending, but always through institutions — never directly to individuals
Why It Failed
  • The “red line”: associationalism’s logic forbade direct federal relief — voluntary cooperation cannot be forced, and suffering individuals cannot wait
  • Will Rogers: “money trickles up, not down” — RFC helped the wealthy while ignoring individual suffering
  • Bonus Army (1932): MacArthur disobeyed Hoover’s orders and drove into the camp with tear gas and tanks; image of soldiers attacking veterans was fatal
  • Hoovervilles: the mocking name itself showed how completely the public blamed him personally
  • The Depression was simply too deep for voluntary measures — associationalism assumed good-faith cooperation that collapse had destroyed
Conclusion
  • Hoover’s philosophy was coherent and principled, not callous — but the crisis exceeded what voluntary cooperation could address
  • His refusal to cross the red line created the political opening for FDR’s 44-state landslide
  • FDR succeeded precisely by abandoning associationalism’s limits and establishing direct federal-individual relationships

Answers to every term on the official study guide, organized by chapter.

Chapter 16 Terms

  • Warren G. Harding: 29th president (1921–23). Promised 'Return to Normalcy' after WWI chaos. Made good appointments (Mellon, Taft, Hoover at Commerce) but terrible ones (Fall, Forbes, Daugherty). Died Aug 2, 1923 in San Francisco before the full scope of the scandals emerged. Reputation destroyed posthumously.
  • John Maynard Keynes: British economist at Paris Peace Conference (1919). Warned Versailles reparations were impossibly high and would produce a second catastrophic war. Compared Wilson to a provincial Presbyterian minister overmatched by European counterparts. Later admired Hoover's Belgian relief work.
  • Post WWI Economy: Postwar dislocation: inflation, labor strikes, farm depression, sharp recession 1920–21. Economy recovered strongly under Harding/Coolidge — grew 42% through the 1920s, national debt halved, average income rose from $6,460 to $8,016.
  • Race Riots: Summer 1919 ('Red Summer'): race riots in dozens of cities including Chicago and Washington D.C. Part of the wave of postwar chaos (flu, Red Scare, economic upheaval) that made Harding's normalcy message so broadly appealing.
  • Spanish Influenza: 1918–1919: killed 675,000 Americans and 22 million worldwide. About 28% of U.S. infected. Spread amplified by wartime troop movements. Philadelphia dug mass graves with steam shovels — 528 buried in a single day.
  • Bolshevik Revolution – Fear of Communism – A. Mitchell Palmer: Bolshevik Revolution (Russia, 1917) triggered fears of communist revolution in the U.S. A.G. A. Mitchell Palmer launched raids after June 1919 bombings (one at his own home), arresting ~6,000 radical aliens. Discredited when his predicted May Day 1920 uprising never materialized.
  • Andrew W. Mellon Policies: Treasury Secretary under Harding, Coolidge, and Hoover. Cut top rate 77%→24%, bottom rate 4%→0.5%. Argued that very high rates caused wealthy to shelter capital, actually reducing revenue — lower rates would paradoxically produce more. Halved national debt from $33B to $16B.
  • Auto Industry and Ford: Henry Ford: Model T ($300), moving assembly line, 9,000 cars/day by 1925. Paid workers double competitors; 40-hour workweek. 26 million cars on roads by 1929. Transformed America — suburbs, road trips, new freedoms, entirely new way of life.
  • New Inventions & Technology: Radio (KDKA Pittsburgh, 1920 — first U.S. broadcast station; 60% of families owned radios by 1929), talking movies, refrigerators, washing machines. Mass consumer culture emerged, fueled by advertising and installment buying (buying on credit).
  • Roaring Twenties: Era of prosperity and mass culture — but McClay warns this is largely a 'gargoyle journalism' myth based on upper-class urban experience. Most Americans lived more like Booth Tarkington's Midwesterners than Fitzgerald's glamorous characters. Real prosperity was uneven — farmers and industrial workers struggled.
  • Leisure: Rising incomes and shorter workweeks created new leisure time. Movies, radio, spectator sports (Babe Ruth), automobiles, tourism expanded. Mass entertainment became a defining feature of American life for the first time.
  • Aviation & Lindbergh: Charles Lindbergh: first solo transatlantic flight, NY→Paris, May 1927. 3,600 miles, 33.5 hours, Spirit of St. Louis, $25,000 prize. 100,000 in Paris; 200,000 in NYC. Classic example of mass media's power to create national mythology — Boorstin's 'pseudo-event.'
  • Creel Committee: Committee on Public Information (CPI), headed by George Creel during WWI. Used propaganda posters, pamphlets, and 'Four Minute Men' speakers to build war support. Demonstrated the power of mass media to shape public opinion — a precursor to 1920s advertising and celebrity culture.
  • Frederick Lewis Allen: Author of Only Yesterday (1931), a popular history of the 1920s. McClay (via Kenneth S. Lynn) criticizes it as 'gargoyle journalism' — based on upper-class urban experience (Fitzgerald's world), not the average American (Tarkington's Midwest). Created the misleading 'revolution in manners and morals' image of the decade.
  • F. Scott Fitzgerald: Novelist who embodied Jazz Age glamour (The Great Gatsby). His world of wealthy parties and social anxiety was the basis for much of the 'Roaring Twenties' mythology. McClay notes this was not representative of most Americans' experience — gargoyle journalism at its finest.
  • Fundamentalism: Religious movement that rejected Darwinian evolution and defended literal Biblical interpretation. Scopes Trial (1925) was its biggest public defeat. McClay notes fundamentalism was not simply ignorant — Bryan had legitimate concerns about Social Darwinism's connection to eugenics and racism.
  • William Jennings Bryan: Three-time Democratic presidential nominee; Secretary of State under Wilson; fundamentalist Christian. Prosecuted the Scopes Trial against Clarence Darrow. Died five days after the trial ended. His concerns about evolution were partly rooted in its connection to eugenics — the textbook Scopes used was openly racist and eugenicist.
  • Scopes Trial and Darwin: 1925, Dayton, TN. John Scopes deliberately violated the Butler Act (banned teaching evolution) to create a test case. Bryan vs. Clarence Darrow; Mencken covered it for 200 national reporters. Scopes convicted but fundamentalism suffered decisive PR defeat. Key complication: Hunter's A Civic Biology (Scopes's textbook) was overtly racist and eugenicist.
  • 18th & 19th Amendments: 18th: Prohibition. Passed Dec 1917, in effect Jan 1919. A Progressive (not reactionary) measure. Alcohol fell 50% but spawned Al Capone and organized crime. Real damage: created laws millions refused to obey, eroding respect for law itself. 19th: Women's suffrage, ratified 1920. Women gained the right to vote nationally.
  • Harding Scandals: Teapot Dome: Interior Sec. Albert Fall leased Teapot Dome (WY) and Elk Hills (CA) oil reserves for $400K bribe — first cabinet official imprisoned. Charles Forbes (Veterans Bureau): massive theft and mismanagement. Harry Daugherty (AG): fraudulent release of German wartime assets. All emerged after Harding's death in 1923.
  • Coolidge to Hoover: Coolidge chose not to run in 1928, preserving his reputation before the crash. Hoover won easily over Al Smith (first Catholic nominee, signaling a new Democratic coalition forming). Hoover: Iowa orphan → Stanford engineer → Belgian relief hero → Commerce Secretary → President. Believed in 'associationalism.' Widely admired — until the Depression.

Chapter 17 Terms

  • Decline of Democratic Party: Democrats deeply divided in the 1920s: urban vs. rural, Catholic vs. Protestant, wet vs. dry, North vs. South. The 1924 convention couldn't agree to condemn the KKK (took 103 ballots to pick a candidate). Al Smith lost badly in 1928. But beneath the surface, a new urban-immigrant-labor coalition was forming that FDR would mobilize in 1932.
  • 1929 and the Crash: Black Thursday (Oct 24) + Black Tuesday (Oct 29). 16.5 million shares traded; prices collapsed. U.S. Steel $262→$22; Dow 381→41 over three years. The crash was a herald of the Depression, not its principal cause — underlying weaknesses (farm crisis, overproduction, easy credit, weak European economies, shaky banks) already existed.
  • Great Depression and Stats: By 1932: 13 million unemployed (25% of workforce). National income fell 40%. Thousands of banks failed. Hoovervilles appeared everywhere. Farm prices collapsed. Real GDP fell ~30%. Worst economic crisis in American history. The Depression did not end until WWII mobilization — New Deal policies never got unemployment below 10%.
  • Progressives vs. Laissez Faire: Hoover rejected both pure laissez-faire ('do nothing') AND direct government intervention. His 'associationalism' was a middle path — voluntary cooperation. Critics on the left (FDR, Long) wanted more direct action. Conservatives wanted less government. Hoover satisfied neither and appeared paralyzed, even though he was more activist than any prior president facing a crisis.
  • 'Associationalism': Hoover's governing philosophy: voluntary cooperation between business, government, and civic organizations. Worked reasonably in prosperity but was inadequate for the Depression's scale. His absolute refusal to cross into direct federal-individual relief (believing it would destroy self-reliance and break the constitutional order) was the fatal limit that doomed his presidency.
  • Hawley-Smoot Tariff: Signed by Hoover in 1930 despite calling it 'obnoxious.' Raised duties on 1,000+ items by up to 49%. Europe retaliated. U.S. exports and imports both cut in half. A body blow to already-struggling world economies. Widely seen as a major factor deepening the Depression globally.
  • FDR and the 'New Deal': FDR won 1932 in a 44-state landslide (57–40%). First Inaugural Mar 4, 1933: 'The only thing we have to fear is fear itself.' Declared bank holiday; launched fireside chats. Hundred Days (Mar–Jun 1933): unprecedented wave of legislation. Holmes's verdict: 'second-class intellect, first-class temperament' — and the temperament was exactly what was needed.
  • Acts and Acronyms: FDIC: insured bank deposits to $5,000. CCC: 3M young men in forests/parks. CWA: 4M workers on roads/bridges. WPA: 8.5M+ employed (including artists/writers). NRA/NIRA: industry codes, blue eagle — struck down Schechter 1935. AAA: paid farmers to reduce production — struck down 1936. TVA: government electricity for Tennessee Valley. SEC: regulated stock markets. FHA: insured home loans. SSA: Social Security 1935.
  • Thunder on the Left: Three major left-wing threats pushing FDR toward the more radical Second New Deal: (1) Huey Long — Share Our Wealth Society, 7.5M members, guaranteed income, assassinated Sept 1935. (2) Father Coughlin — ~45M radio listeners, currency reform, increasingly antisemitic. (3) Dr. Townsend — $200/month for all elderly, directly inspired Social Security's old-age provisions.
  • Social Security Act: 1935. FDR's 'cornerstone.' Retirement pensions funded by equal payroll taxes on BOTH worker and employer — FDR insisted on this so recipients would feel they had earned and paid into the system, not received charity. Also included state-federal unemployment insurance and aid for disabled and dependent children. First direct federal-individual financial relationship in U.S. history.
  • Court Packing Scheme: 1937. FDR proposed adding one new justice for every sitting justice over 70 (up to 6 new justices). Goal: dilute conservatives who struck down NIRA and AAA. Fiercely resisted by both parties as an assault on judicial independence. FDR backed down — but two justices apparently shifted positions anyway ('switch in time that saved nine'), upholding Wagner Act and Social Security.
  • Recession of '37: Sharp downturn caused by three factors: (1) FDR's budget-cutting to balance the budget, (2) $2B drain of new Social Security payroll taxes on consumer spending, (3) business anxiety over Wagner Act and ongoing New Deal regulation. Economy dropped below early-1930s depths. Fueled the argument that New Deal policies prolonged rather than cured the Depression.
  • Elections of '38: Democrats lost 6 Senate seats, 71 House seats, 12 governorships. Conservative coalition of Republicans and conservative southern Democrats took effective control of the House. Arthur Krock (NY Times): 'The New Deal has been halted.' No major New Deal legislation passed after 1938. The Depression ultimately ended only with WWII mobilization — 'not the moral equivalent of war, but war itself.'
